# Board Briefing: UAE AI Regulatory Requirements for Banking & Fintech

> **Audience: Board of Directors, Board Risk Committee, Audit Committee**
> **Reading time: 5 minutes**
> **Date: April 2026**

---

## The Situation in Plain Terms

The Central Bank of UAE (CBUAE) has issued binding requirements on how licensed financial institutions must govern, deploy, and use artificial intelligence. These requirements are **already in effect** and will form part of CBUAE examinations. The national UAE AI Charter sets the ethical framework that all of this builds upon.

This is not a future compliance project. It is a current examination risk.

---

## What Has Been Issued

| Document | Issued By | Status | What It Does |
|----------|-----------|--------|--------------|
| **Guidance Note on Consumer Protection and Responsible AI** | CBUAE | February 2026 — in effect | Sets supervisory expectations on human oversight, explainability, fairness, and customer disclosures for AI |
| **Rulebook: Model Management Standards (MMS)** | CBUAE | In force (foundational) | Governs the full lifecycle of AI and analytical models: documentation, validation, monitoring, and accountability |
| **UAE AI Charter** | UAE Government | June 2024 — actively cited | Establishes eight national ethical principles for AI that regulators reference and boards must adopt |

---

## What the Board Is Personally Responsible For

Under these frameworks, the Board cannot disclaim AI risk. The following are **Board-level obligations** — not delegable to management alone:

1. **Approve the AI Risk Policy** — The institution must have a Board-approved AI Risk Policy that incorporates UAE AI Charter principles. If this policy does not exist or is out of date, the Board is exposed.

2. **Establish Governance Oversight** — The Board is ultimately accountable for AI systems that affect consumers. An AI/Model Governance Committee must be in place, with formal Terms of Reference, reporting ultimately to the Board or Board Risk Committee.

3. **Receive Regular AI Risk Reporting** — The Board must receive formal AI risk reporting at least annually (quarterly is recommended). AI risk cannot only live in management committees.

4. **Accountability Cannot Be Delegated** — "We were not aware" is not a supervisory defence. CBUAE examiners will check Board minutes for evidence that AI risk was actively discussed.

---

## The Five Things Every Board Member Must Know

**1. What AI systems does the institution operate?**
Every model and AI system must be registered in a central Model Inventory. If the institution does not have a complete, current inventory, that is a critical examination gap — and a governance failure.

**2. Are high-impact AI decisions overseen by humans?**
For decisions that significantly affect customers — credit approvals, AML flags, fraud determinations, product eligibility — there must be a mechanism for a human to review the AI's decision. Customers must be told when AI made a decision about them and must be able to request human review.

**3. Have high-risk AI models been independently validated?**
The highest-risk models (Tier 1) must be validated by a team independent from the people who built them. Validation must produce a formal opinion. Outstanding critical findings cannot remain unmitigated while the model runs in production.

**4. Are we telling customers when AI is making decisions about them?**
This is a specific, testable requirement. Chatbots must identify themselves as AI. Adverse decisions driven by AI must trigger customer notifications. Customers must be able to get plain-language explanations of those decisions.

**5. Are we responsible for vendor AI too?**
Yes. If the institution deploys AI built by a third party — including large language models from technology vendors — the institution is fully responsible for that AI. "We bought it from a vendor" is not a valid defence.

## The Three Questions the Board Should Ask Management Now

> **These questions should be asked at the next Board or Risk Committee meeting and answered with documentary evidence, not verbal assurance.**

---

### Question 1: "Show me our Model Inventory."

**What a good answer looks like:**
Management presents a current Model Inventory listing all AI and ML systems in use across the institution. Each entry shows the model name, business purpose, owner, risk tier (Tier 1, 2, or 3), and validation status. The inventory has been attested by each business unit as complete.

**Red flags:**
- "We're still building it"
- "It's in someone's spreadsheet"
- Inventory exists but was last updated more than 6 months ago
- High-impact credit and fraud models are missing

---

### Question 2: "Which Tier 1 models have had independent validation in the past 12 months, and what did validation find?"

**What a good answer looks like:**
Management presents validation reports for the institution's most critical AI systems. Reports were produced by a team independent from the developers. Each report has a formal opinion (Approve / Approve with Conditions / Reject). Outstanding findings are tracked in a remediation log with owners and due dates.

**Red flags:**
- No independent validation function exists
- Validation was done by the same team that built the model
- Critical findings exist but no remediation plan is in place
- Models have not been validated within their required review cycle

---

### Question 3: "How do we tell customers that AI made a decision about them, and what can they do about it?"

**What a good answer looks like:**
Management shows the consumer disclosure language in product terms and the mobile app. They demonstrate the notification process for adverse decisions. They explain the human review request workflow — how customers can request it, what the SLA is, and how outcomes are logged. AI complaints are a named category in the complaints system, and data on these complaints has been reported to the Governance Committee.

**Red flags:**
- No disclosure language exists
- Chatbots do not identify themselves as AI
- No process exists for customers to request human review
- AI complaints are not separately tracked

## Examination Risk Assessment

| Examiner Focus Area | Risk if Not Addressed | Timeline to Address |
|--------------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| Missing Model Inventory | Critical finding; potential restrictions on AI deployment | Immediate (Month 1) |
| No Board AI Governance structure | Critical finding; remediation order likely | Immediate (Month 1) |
| No Tier 1 model validation | Critical finding; potential model suspension | Urgent (Month 3–6) |
| No consumer AI disclosures | High finding; consumer protection violation | Urgent (Month 3–6) |
| No human review mechanism for high-impact AI | High finding; consumer protection breach | Urgent (Month 3–6) |
| Vendor AI with no due diligence | High finding; third-party risk failure | Medium (Month 6–9) |
| No fairness testing | High finding; potential enforcement | Medium (Month 6–9) |
| No AI complaints category | Medium finding | Medium (Month 4–6) |

## Board Resolution Recommended

At the next scheduled Board or Board Risk Committee meeting, the Board should formally:

1. **Receive and note** a management briefing on AI regulatory requirements under the three frameworks
2. **Approve** (or direct management to finalise for approval) the institution's AI Risk Policy incorporating UAE AI Charter principles
3. **Confirm** the designation of a named Responsible AI Officer at Senior Management level
4. **Direct** management to complete a Model Inventory within 60 days and present it to the next Board Risk Committee
5. **Schedule** quarterly AI risk reporting to the Board from the next reporting cycle
